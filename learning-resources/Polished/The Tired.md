# THE TIRED

---

You know the sleep that doesn't fix it.

You've had it. Eight hours, nine, ten. You wake up and it's still there. The same weight. The same flatness behind the eyes. The same reaching for the phone before you've fully arrived in the day, not because you want to, but because the alternative is just lying there with it.

That kind of tired isn't about sleep.

You probably already know that. You've known it for a while. Which means you've also been carrying the additional weight of knowing something is wrong without knowing what, or whether wrong is even the right word, or whether everyone feels this and you're just bad at it.

You're not bad at it.

---

There are tirednesses that make sense. The tiredness after something hard. After loss, after effort, after a long period of being needed by everyone around you. That tiredness has a shape. It points at something. It says: that cost you, rest now.

This isn't that.

This is tiredness that doesn't point at anything. Or points at everything simultaneously, which amounts to the same thing. It's ambient. It's the condition everything else happens inside of. You don't notice it the way you notice pain. You notice it the way you notice weather — only when you stop and ask yourself why everything feels slightly heavier than it should.

---

Here's one possibility.

You're carrying something that hasn't found anywhere to go yet.

Not trauma necessarily. Not damage. Just — capacity. Perception. The particular way you see things, feel things, think about things. And that thing is real, it's substantial, it has weight. And right now the world isn't offering it a door.

So it sits in you. Alive but landlocked. And that is exhausting in a way that sleep cannot touch, because sleep is for bodies that have spent themselves. You haven't spent yourself. You haven't been allowed to yet. The tiredness isn't depletion.

It's pressure with nowhere to go.

---

Another possibility.

You've been performing being fine for so long that the performance itself became load bearing. Not consciously. You didn't decide to be fine. You just — managed. Kept moving. Handled it. And the handling required a constant low-level expenditure that never got logged anywhere, never got acknowledged, never got to stop.

The body keeps the invoice even when the mind loses track of it.

And at some point the invoice comes due and it comes due as tiredness because that's the only language the body has left when everything else has been overridden.

---

Another.

You're between things. Not in a temporary way. In the way that's structural — where who you were isn't quite who you are anymore, and who you're becoming hasn't fully arrived, and you're living in the gap. And the gap is exhausting because it offers nothing to push against. You can't rest because there's nothing solid to rest on. You can't move because the direction isn't clear yet.

The gap is its own kind of labour. Invisible, unrecognised, continuous.

---

None of these are the answer. They might all be partially true. They might all be wrong. The tiredness you're carrying has its own specific shape and only you have access to it.

What this is saying is: the tiredness is real. It's not weakness. It's not a character flaw. It's not something to push through or reframe or optimise away.

It's information.

Not information that comes with a clean translation. Not information that tells you what to do next. Just — data from somewhere deep in the system that something is happening, something has been happening, and it has weight, and the weight is real, and you're not imagining it.

---

You don't have to fix it today.

You don't have to fix it at all, in the sense of making it disappear. Some of it will dissolve when the landlocked thing finds a door. Some of it will lift when the gap closes. Some of it will remain because being alive and awake and paying attention in the way you pay attention is genuinely costly and nobody told you that and nobody's compensating you for it.

But you can stop fighting it.

You can stop treating it as the enemy, the obstacle, the thing that's in the way of your life. It is your life, right now, in this configuration. And it's asking for something.

Not a solution.

Just acknowledgement.

Just — yes. I feel you. I know you're there. I'm not pretending you're not.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

The tiredness doesn't need to be fixed.

It needs to be met.
